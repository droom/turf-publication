Desire Paths Publication Application
Chris Droom / Norwood Letters

A desire path is a route “where the grass is worn thin between the places we find ourselves and the places we would like to be”. Deviating from controlled routes designed by urban planning, property ownership and traffic systems, they symbolise the self-made solutions carved out by communities (human or animal), the wisdom of crowds, intuitive design & rebellious creativity. Desire Paths is Turf Projects’ Croydon London Borough of Culture flagship project and part of Turf’s year exploring the theme ‘DEMYSTIFICATION’. A satellite arts festival across Croydon, the project aims to explore how communities and creatives can shape the spaces and places around them, with a focus on disused and underused spaces around the borough. An artist-led charity founded by Croydon locals in 2013, Turf was set up to provide opportunities & space for creatives locally. Operating in ex-retail spaces in Croydon with an uncertain future due to as-yet-unrealised urban development, the questions we frequently found ourselves asking about who has power over space in our hometown acted as our starting point for Desire Paths;

  1. How do spaces end up empty? How can we bring them back into use?
  2. How can people be more actively involved in these processes, why aren’t they?
  3. Who owns space in Croydon? What are the roles of culture, commerce & power in how space is shaped, valued and co-opted?
  4. How does rapid change & regeneration impact our sense of wellbeing, stability, belonging & collective memory?
  5. How could Croydon forge its own path in determining how spaces and places are used?

We wanted to approach these questions with humour, playfulness and curiosity, in an attempt to demystify the processes behind them. Desire Paths will be led by artists, organisations or collectives chosen by open call. The project will start with a symposium event at Turf and then unfold and expand across Croydon via the projects commissioned through this open call.

Norwood Letters

In 2023 I started Norwood Letters, a research-led design practice, focussed on the local community. This arose from a few themes which have been on my mind for some time:

  1. Belonging, homelands; the rising-tide of nationalism
  2. The co-ordinated attacks on education, and the success of anti-intellectualism as a dominant lens for which to understand current and past events
  3. Environments—in particular relation to mental health and well-being

Norwood Letters has three lofty ambitions:

  1. To uplift communities
  2. To share knowledge
  3. To change culture
Although great new ideas are usually articulated by individuals, they’re nearly always generated by communities. Being an artist, you hear a lot of talk about genius; which is the process of singling out certain people in art history and saying, “those were the important ones,” ... Whenever you look at any of those artists, you find that they lived and drew from a very, very active, flourishing cultural scene. And they were only one of the elements in that scene. So just as genius is the creative intelligence of an individual, scenius is the creative intelligence of a community.
— Brian Eno

Between 2020–2022 I co-ran a digital co-operative as Design Lead, and played a key role in developing collaborative processes, both for internal and external teams. Many of these were adapted during the pandemic for remote working. Below are some of the methods I would try to apply to working with with large groups in the research and collaboration of creating the publication:

  1. Using Miro whiteboarding, we can easily generate ideas even with large teams. I had often used this during discovery sessions with clients, the largest meeting being with IPPR, which numbered around 15 people on the call, all contributing.
  2. I work with Figma which has a feedback tool built-in, and is freely available to use. What I've found works really well is creating lo-fi scamps for layout and rough content, to then be designed in a desktop publisher (For me, InDesign).
  3. By applying Agile to the design process we can created with structured feedback windows and crits
  4. Sociocracy in decision-making

Through a project started this year (South Norwood Visual Arts), I've been lucky to have met loads of great people in the area, many of whom are involved in community projects. I would be happy to be connected with other applicants if skills / interests compliment each other, and have experience working like this.

Documentation

I think the publication will require careful documentation. I like to keep a cloud-based research documents for every project, which has lots of benefits for collaboration. These documents are always kept orderly and time-stamped, and can be made accessible to anyone working on the project. These house call notes, background research, serve as a record of thinking / decisions made along the way, and ideas to potentially explore at a later date.

Imprint

In the last few years I began writing/designing two small books: an oral history charting my parents' troubled relationship and its effects, and a compilation of design writing, mainly focussed on environments.

Sharing Knowledge

Sharing knowledge and exposing the process of design is something I think is really important to do where possible. This means explaining techniques and process to add to 'the commons' of both collaborative working and graphic design.

Since 2022, I've found a lot of joy in the local history section at South Norwood and Croydon libraries, mainly in regard to geographies, design history, and oral histories. I think there is a lot of importance in documenting and preserving memories and spaces, inviting curiosity and showcasing Croydon as a place of creativity, folklore and culture. 'Space' and 'culture' is something I believe the community shapes, rather than it being a top-down endeavour.
